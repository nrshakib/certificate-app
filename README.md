Letter of Motivation

To
The Admissions Committee
Universität zu Lübeck
Germany

Subject: Application for the Master of Science in Robotics and Autonomous Systems

Dear Members of the Admissions Committee,

I am writing to apply for the **M.Sc. in Robotics and Autonomous Systems** at Universität zu Lübeck. My interest in this field grew gradually — not from a single moment of inspiration, but from years of working on AI systems and repeatedly running into the same limitation: software intelligence that works well in controlled conditions tends to fall apart when it has to deal with the physical world. I want to understand that problem at a deeper level, and this program is where I can do that.

I completed my **Bachelor of Science in Computer Science and Engineering** from **Daffodil International University**, graduating with a **CGPA of 3.61 out of 4.00**. The degree covered a wide range, but what stayed with me most was the mathematical foundation — not because the exams were hard, but because those subjects kept reappearing in my professional work in ways I didn't expect. I studied calculus, linear algebra, complex variables, and coordinate geometry, followed by ordinary and partial differential equations and engineering mathematics in later semesters. Statistics and probability, and numerical methods, completed a foundation that I now recognize as essential for almost everything in autonomous systems — **state estimation, motion prediction, optimization, and sensor modeling** all trace back to this groundwork.

The informatics and hardware-side coursework was equally important. Courses in data structures, algorithms, computer architecture, and digital electronics gave me a working understanding of how computation actually runs — not just in theory but under constraints. **Microprocessor and Assembly Language** took that further; writing code at that level changes how you think about performance and control in a way that higher-level programming does not. I also completed coursework in data communication, computer networks, operating systems, and compiler design. Individually these seem like standard CS curriculum. Together, they built an instinct for **systems thinking** — understanding how components interact, where failures propagate, and what reliability actually requires.

In the final years of my degree, my coursework moved toward intelligent and physical systems. **Artificial Intelligence** introduced me to the formal side of decision-making under uncertainty. **Introduction to Robotics** gave that a physical dimension — suddenly the question wasn't just "what should the system decide" but "how does it act on that decision in a real environment." **Embedded Systems** and **Computer Graphics** were both unexpectedly useful: the former for understanding real-time control constraints, the latter for developing spatial intuition around perception and visual representation. **Simulation and Modeling** tied several threads together by showing how analytical models and computational experiments can substitute for physical trials during early design phases.

Professionally, I work as a **Software Engineer** building **Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) systems**. These are AI pipelines that pull from multiple external knowledge sources to keep language model outputs accurate and grounded in real data. The engineering challenge sounds narrow but isn't: you are constantly dealing with incomplete information, conflicting sources, strict latency requirements, and the need for the system to remain reliable even when inputs are noisy or unexpected. That experience sharpened my thinking about a broader class of problems — the same class, I've come to believe, that autonomous systems face. The difference is that in robotics, **an unreliable inference doesn't just produce a wrong answer; it produces a physical consequence.** I want to work on problems where that distinction matters.

Universität zu Lübeck stands out to me for a specific reason. The **Institute of Robotics and Cognitive Systems** has not just studied clinical robotics — it has deployed it. Radiosurgery systems developed there have now treated over **130,000 cancer patients**, operating under constraints that most robotics research never has to meet: **sub-millimeter precision, real-time motion compensation, and inference that cannot be wrong.** That kind of applied rigor is exactly what I am looking for. The interdisciplinary structure of the institute — bringing together computer science, medicine, mathematics, and engineering around shared problems — reflects how I think serious work in this field has to be done.

I want to be straightforward about my starting point: I do not have graduate-level experience in control theory or medical robotics. What I do bring is a solid technical foundation, genuine professional experience handling the kinds of data and inference problems that clinical robotics depends on, and a clear sense of where I want to develop. This program is how I close that gap seriously, not just on a transcript.

After completing the M.Sc., I intend to pursue **doctoral research in embodied intelligence**, with a particular focus on autonomous systems in clinical and high-stakes environments. The master's degree is not an end goal — it is the stage where I build the theoretical depth and research experience to do that work properly.

Thank you for your time and consideration. I would be glad to contribute to the research and academic environment at Universität zu Lübeck.

Yours sincerely,
Mahmudul Hasan
